Abstract

Throughout this project, I tried to see if there was a quantifiable way that one could use to justify a move to a different country. Using the World Happiness Report’s data as a basis, I reverse engineered their equation for six variables to determine a country’s happiness on a 0 to 10 scale. From there, I subbed in more objective variables and fine tuned the equation. Further research could be done with more expansive datasets. However, I’m happy with the fact that I got an equation that works for the most part.

Introduction

In looking at data analysis projects that other people had done for inspiration, I stumbled across the World Happiness Report. I skimmed through the incredibly long document, and had fun poking around the data they provided. However, I wondered about other factors that could matter more than Generosity (one variable in the WHR). What if it really mattered to me that healthcare is great in the country? There was no equation for it, though. I figured out I would have to make my own.

Every year, the researchers behind the World Happiness Report redo the surveys to see how the world has changed. Every year, they have to change the equation. These past couple, they’ve tried to see how COVID-19 had an impact on countries’ happiness. Turns out, people can still be happy when there’s a pandemic. Typically, even though they change the equation, they keep the variables the same. They have evolved over time, but now they’ve sort of locked in the variables they like to use and that matter to people. The goal of my ‘research’ is to see if I can reverse engineer the ladder scores from the World Happiness Report dataset and fit other variables into it. I’m not expecting perfection, but something that produces reasonable results would be nice.

Detailing the Datasets

World Happiness Report

The first dataset I got was from Gallup’s World Happiness Report 2023. The factors it had included ladder score, GDP per capita, Social support, Healthy life expectancy, Freedom to make Life Choices, Generosity, and Perceptions of corruption. GDP per capita is relatively simple. Social Support is the percentage of people who can say that they have someone to rely on. Healthy life expectancy is about how long healthy people typically live. Freedom to make life choices is the percentage of people who feel in control of their destiny. Generosity is the percentage of people who had donated to a charity within the last month. Perceptions of corruption is the only variable we want to be low–it’s the percentage of people who think their government is immoral. There was also the variable ‘Dystopia + residual,’ which served as a regression balance. It was sort of like how much the country differed from the worst country imaginable.

Cantril’s ladder is a measurement of life satisfaction. Respondents are asked to imagine a ladder where 0 is the bottom and 10 is the top. They then rank their life based on the rung they think they’re on. The average ladder score in a country is what the World Happiness Report is based on. In addition to the variables mentioned previously, there are also columns that list ‘Explained by: [insert variable here].’ The researchers attempted to attribute proportions of the ladder score to each of the six variables. The variables are listed generally in order of proportion. In some cases, Social support outranks Logged GDP, which is why the researchers said they weren’t able to make one equation that would work for every country. After all, societies differ, and some value family and friendship more than economic health. That said, I still tried to come up with an equation that could roughly do the same thing.

Kaggle’s Global Country Information

The second dataset I used, I found on Kaggle. I think some guy, Nidula Elgiriyewithana, just made it. It originally had 34 variables, but I narrowed it down to numerical ones. Official language and country abbreviation wouldn’t work for this project. Here is a breakdown of the variables I did keep in and adjust name-wise (also seen in the accompanying Shiny dashboard):

Agricultural Land: the percentage of the country used for ag purposes

Forested Area: % of the country that is forest

GDP: GDP per capita (I did adjust this one myself. It came as total GDP, so I divided it by the included population.)

Primary Education: gross enrollment ratio for primary education

Tertiary Education: gross enrollment ratio for tertiary education”),

Life expectancy: healthy life expectancy

Physicians: # of physicians per 1k people

Labor Force: % of pop. in the labor force

Co2 Emissions: Co2 emissions in tons

CPI: Consumer Price Index

CPI Change: % change of CPI from the last year

Infant mortality: for every 1,000 births, how many children under 1 year of age died

Mother mortality: # of maternal deaths per 100k births

Out of Pocket Health Expenditures: % of total health costs paid out of pocket

Unemployment Rate: % of labor force that’s unemployed

Density: people per km^2

Armed Forces: size of the country’s Armed Forces

Fertility: average # of kids a woman has

Urban population: % of pop. living in urban areas

Process

The first dataset

First off, I took out any extreme outliers and countries with N/A values out of the dataset. While they do matter, they made my process harder. It’s not like I’m getting paid for this, so I made my life easier. Sue me. I divided Healthy life expectancy by 100 so I could have it on a 0-1 scale, and did the same to GDP, but divided by 11.66, which was the max. This made the equation finding simpler. Then, I found the mean of each of the six variables. I tried to use that in the equation, but as it turns out, that was useless. What I needed to do was find the mean of the columns that said ‘Explained by: [insert variable here]’ I more or less used those as the coefficients in my equation. I multiplied the GDP factor by 11.66 to adjust for its weight in the equation since I had initially divided by that. Below are the coefficients I started messing around with:

##      Mean      Mean      Mean      Mean      Mean      Mean 
## 1.4145727 1.1687363 3.6796985 0.5480548 0.1480416 0.1501618

When I left the coefficients as they were above, my equations were far too narrow. I played around with multiplying the factors with different numbers. I multiplied Social support and Perceptions of corruption both by 2.5, and Healthy life expectancy and Generosity by 0.5. This made the values way too high, though. I then divided the score in half and subtracted each country’s Dystopia + residual score. This gave me a range of equation scores that were sufficiently large. Granted, the minimum and maximum were a little on either side of the regular scores, but at that point, I would have rather had a wider range than totally incorrect scores. This got me satisfactory summary statistics, as seen below.

Then I moved on to my second dataset.

The second dataset

With this one, my first step was taking out the non-numerical variables. From there. I coerced the variables to the numerical class, since they had been characters. With the variables I kept, I divided them by the maximum value in their column. This was to get them on a 0-1 scale like the first dataset. I organized them into ones you would want high, low, and either, but then realized that not everyone follows my brain pattern. I essentially duplicated the variables, but did (1-[variable]) to get it to the other desire. For example, if someone hated babies and wanted infant mortality high, they could have that. For factors like Density and Armed Forces, I created two: the initial value was ‘WH’ (want high), and (1-[variable]) was ‘WL’ (want low).

Now, the second dataset had more countries than the first one. This happened because the researchers for the World Happiness Report couldn’t get sufficient data from every country that you can gather simple statistics on. This made me unable to use each country’s individual ‘Dystopia + residual’ score. The average for it was around 1.775, so I instead simply subtracted that flat value when translating the formula for use on the larger dataset, as well as dividing by 2 like the original equation I had come up with.

I can’t show summary statistics for this one because of the fundamental goal. I wanted to create an equation that people could sub their own values in for. Because there are so many combinations of variables you could place in, it would look disgusting and take forever if I were to show summary statistics for every combination. So: trust, bro.

TL;DR

In case you couldn’t understand the rest of this report, here’s a TL;DR.

I used the World Happiness Report to work backwards to an equation that I could put my own variables into, because I know better than Gallup researchers. I used their variables to find coefficients that I could then multiply by values I found in a different dataset. In the accompanying dashboard, users can pick 6 variables that are important to them, and it will spit out a result using that equation that calculates a score rating their probable happiness level on a scale from 0-10.

Limitations

The main limitation here is that I am not a super rigorous statistician or researcher. I did a lot of unnecessary steps (that I have not included in this report) and probably went overboard in getting variables where I wanted them. In the future, it might be better to develop multiple equations based on societal factors. (Remember that I mentioned above that some countries’ ladder scores were more explained by Social support than GDP, for example.) However, I am also not an anthropologist. I don’t think my method of culturally separating countries would be fair or representative, and subsetting them by continents wouldn’t account for the breadth of diversity.

Another important limitation is that I only used two datasets. In the future, it would likely provide data that could stand up better and be closer to the fact if I corroborated all of these values. In this case, I wanted a simple dataset that I wouldn’t have to fit against other datasets and deal with non-equal values. My b.

Ways to Further this Project

Another way I could have improved this work is finding more fun variables to use. Most of the values in the first dataset were subjective, which was the whole point of their project. I went in the opposite direction with the second dataset, focusing on objective factors. I could have found more important ones, like the presence of multigenerational households, average vacation days, or average number of pets. I also could have/should have found a way to quantify the values in the second dataset like official language spoken, so I could reflect how that might be important for some.

Conclusion

The equation isn’t foolproof, obviously. It’s not even me-proof. It went down a lot of stupid paths that I then had to back up from. There are some ways that you can make it look stupid, like if you picked variables that no sane or well-meaning person would ever pick (think high infant and mother mortality, high unemployment, low GDP, etc). However, it works most of the time. Small victories.

As for what this means for others, that’s questionable. The smallest way that it matters to other people is that you can test out where you might want to move. On a larger scale, it might show the World Happiness Report that they should make their equation public, or at least the subsetting categories they used. That would’ve made my life easier. You can see above the ways in which one could further this research.

Overall, I had a lot of fun with this. It was interesting to brain dump everything I wanted to do with the code into R and then sort through what was actually viable. I could see myself exploring more data like this outside of this course.
